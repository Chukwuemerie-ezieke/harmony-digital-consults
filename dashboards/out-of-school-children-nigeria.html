Why are children out of school?

UNICEF, UNESCO, and NILDS consistently identify these six drivers in Nigeria:

1. Poverty & child labour

Families need children's labour or cannot afford uniforms, books, and transport — even where tuition is "free".

2. Insecurity

Banditry, insurgency, and school abductions (e.g. Chibok, Dapchi, Kebbi) have closed schools and kept children at home, especially in the North.

3. Early marriage of girls

Girls in North-West and North-East states are often married before 15, ending formal schooling. UNICEF data shows girls are disproportionately affected.

4. Almajiri / informal religious schooling

Millions of boys attend Quranic schools that are not counted as formal education — they remain "out of school" by UNESCO definitions.

5. Weak supply & teacher shortages

Many communities lack a nearby school, qualified teachers, or learning materials — particularly in remote northern LGAs.

6. Disability & exclusion

Children with disabilities face severe access barriers — only a small fraction of schools have inclusive facilities or trained staff.

What the numbers say

  • The crisis is concentrated. Just three states — Katsina, Kano, and Jigawa — account for roughly 16% of all out-of-school children. Targeting interventions geographically is far more cost-effective than national averages suggest.
  • Two numbers, two methodologies. UNICEF's 18.3M figure covers ages 6–15 (primary + lower secondary). UNESCO's 20.2M figure covers a broader 6–18 age band. Both are correct — match the figure to your reporting period.
  • Insecurity has reversed gains. The 2025 abduction of 25 schoolgirls in Kebbi (UNICEF SitRep) shows the problem is not just access but safety. Donor pipelines must now budget for school protection.
  • Girls bear the brunt in the North. In Kebbi and Sokoto, the majority of out-of-school children are girls — pointing to early-marriage and cultural-norm interventions as priority levers.
  • EdTech can reach where schools cannot. Offline-capable digital learning (Kolibri, uLesson Schools offline app, solar-powered tablets) is one of the few interventions that scales in insecure or remote LGAs.
