Unit 3 of 6

Generating Lesson Plans, Schemes of Work & Notes

Turn a single line from your scheme of work into a full lesson plan, a student note, and a set of evaluation questions — in under 10 minutes. Then learn the editing pass that makes it truly yours.

⏱ 60 minutes 📊 Beginner–Intermediate 🎯 4 learning objectives

Learning objectives

  1. Generate a complete lesson plan aligned to the Nigerian (or your local) curriculum.
  2. Produce a weekly scheme of work in under 10 minutes.
  3. Convert lesson plans into student-friendly notes.
  4. Edit and personalise AI output to match your voice and your classroom reality.

Anatomy of a strong lesson plan

Whatever curriculum you teach, a strong lesson plan has the same skeleton:

  • Topic & class level
  • Learning objectives (what learners will be able to do)
  • Prior knowledge (what they already know)
  • Instructional materials (chalk, posters, real objects)
  • Presentation steps (introduction → development → practice)
  • Evaluation (questions and short tasks)
  • Assignment / homework

When you ask AI for a lesson plan, ask for every one of these explicitly. Otherwise you'll get a 4-paragraph essay instead of a plan.

Feed the AI your curriculum

AI doesn't automatically know your scheme of work. Paste it in. Even a single line works:

"Week 4 — JSS1 Basic Science — Living and Non-living Things."

Then ask it to expand:

💡 Prompt template — Scheme to lesson plan

"Act as a JSS1 Basic Science teacher in Nigeria. Expand this scheme entry — 'Week 4 — Living and Non-living Things' — into a full 40-minute lesson plan. Include: learning objectives, prior knowledge, materials, step-by-step presentation, 2 class activities, 5 evaluation questions, and homework. Use a Nigerian context and locally available materials."

Differentiation in one extra line

After your first lesson plan, just say:

  • "Now produce three versions of the practice activity — for struggling learners, average learners, and advanced learners."
  • "Add two extension questions for fast finishers."
  • "Make a simpler version for a Primary 4 class."

One sentence of follow-up replaces 30 minutes of rewriting.

Localisation — making it feel like home

AI defaults to American or generic examples. Force it to localise:

  • "Use Nigerian names — Adaeze, Musa, Tunde — not John and Mary."
  • "Use Naira, not dollars."
  • "Replace the apples with mangoes, cassava, or yams."
  • "Mention boda-boda, danfo, or okada where transport is needed."
  • "Use Yoruba, Hausa, or Igbo words where it fits naturally."

Save these instructions as a reusable "Localisation block" you append to every prompt.

From lesson plan to student note

Once your lesson plan is ready, ask:

"Now turn the content of this lesson plan into a 1-page student note in simple English suitable for a JSS1 pupil. Use short sentences, 3 headings, and a 5-question 'Check yourself' section at the end."

You now have:

  • A teacher's lesson plan ✓
  • A student handout ✓
  • Built-in evaluation questions ✓

All from one scheme entry.

The final edit — always

AI gets things wrong. Common errors to watch for:

  • Foreign examples slipping back in
  • Wrong dates, names, or science facts
  • Misalignment with your specific syllabus
  • Vocabulary above (or below) your class level
  • Activities that need materials you don't have

Rule of thumb: AI drafts; the teacher decides. Spend the saved time reviewing for accuracy and cultural fit — not retyping the whole thing.

🎬 Demo — Scheme line to full lesson kit

Paste one line from your scheme: "Week 4 — JSS1 Basic Science — Living and Non-living Things."

Step 1: Ask AI to expand into a 40-minute lesson plan (with objectives, materials, steps, evaluation, homework). 30 seconds.

Step 2: Say "Now produce a 1-page student note from this plan." 20 seconds.

Step 3: Say "Now give me 5 short evaluation questions with answer key." 15 seconds.

Step 4: You spend 5 minutes editing for local accuracy. Total: under 10 minutes. Old way: 90 minutes.

Unit summary

  • Every lesson plan has the same skeleton — ask AI for every part by name.
  • Paste your scheme of work directly — even one line is enough.
  • Differentiate in one follow-up sentence.
  • Localise aggressively (names, currency, foods, references).
  • AI drafts; the teacher decides. Always edit.
