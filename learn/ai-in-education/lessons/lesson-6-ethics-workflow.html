The five ethical pillars

PillarWhat it means in practice
PrivacyNever input student names, grades, health info, family details, or exam content before the exam.
AccuracyVerify every fact AI produces. AI hallucinates confidently.
TransparencyDisclose AI use to students and parents when appropriate.
EquityMake sure all students benefit, not just those with devices at home.
OriginalityPreserve genuine student work and your own teacher voice.

The "never paste" list

Treat public AI tools (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude on free tiers) like an open café table — assume anyone could see what you put on it. Never paste:

  • Student full names and addresses
  • Student grades attached to names
  • Health, family, or counselling notes
  • Confidential staff information
  • Exam questions before the exam

If you must use real data, anonymise it: replace names with Pupil A, Pupil B; remove all identifiers.

Detecting AI in student work

Tools like GPTZero exist but are unreliable — they produce false positives, especially with non-native English writers. A better approach:

  • Watch for tone shifts: perfect grammar from a usually-struggling student is a flag.
  • Generic content: assignments that feel "too clean" and lack personal voice.
  • Ask follow-up questions: request a verbal defence or oral explanation of one paragraph.
  • Design assessments differently (see next section).

Don't accuse without evidence. Open a conversation, not a court case.

AI-resistant assessment design

Instead of fighting AI in submissions, design tasks AI can't easily complete:

  • In-class timed writing
  • Oral defence of an essay (5 minutes per student)
  • Process portfolios — drafts, sketches, journal entries
  • Hand-drawn diagrams or maps
  • Personal reflection tied to a specific class activity
  • Group presentations with Q&A
  • Practical/lab work with on-the-spot observation

Your weekly AI workflow

The whole point of this course: reclaim hours of your week. Here's a workflow used by experienced AI-using teachers:

DayTaskTime
MondayGenerate the week's lesson plans30 min
TuesdayBuild worksheets and quizzes20 min
WednesdayCreate visuals and slides (Gamma / Canva)20 min
ThursdayDraft parent communications and feedback15 min
FridayReflect, save best prompts, plan next week15 min

Total: 1 hour 40 minutes a week. Compare that to the 10–20 hours many teachers spend now.

Staying current

AI tools change fast. Don't chase every new app. Follow 2–3 trusted sources:

  • MagicSchool AI blog
  • ISTE (International Society for Technology in Education)
  • Common Sense Education
  • Harmony Digital Consults newsletter

30 minutes a month of reading keeps you current.

The Harmony Teacher's AI Pledge

"I pledge to use AI as a tool that supports — never replaces — my professional judgement. I will protect my students' privacy, verify every fact, disclose AI use when appropriate, and continue to develop my own teaching craft. I will use the time AI saves me to give more attention, care, and creativity to my pupils."
