Foundations of AI for Educators

Before you can use AI confidently in your classroom, you need a working mental model of what AI actually is, what it can do well, and where it falls short. This unit gives you that foundation.

⏱ 45 minutes 📊 Beginner 🎯 4 learning objectives

Learning objectives

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

  1. Define Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Generative AI in plain language.
  2. Identify at least 5 AI tools relevant to teaching.
  3. Distinguish between what AI can and cannot do in an educational context.
  4. Recognise the ethical boundaries of AI use in schools.

What is AI, really?

AI is software that recognises patterns, makes predictions, and — increasingly — generates new content. You already use AI dozens of times a day without thinking about it:

  • Autocomplete on your phone keyboard.
  • Google Maps predicting traffic.
  • YouTube recommending the next video.
  • Bank apps flagging unusual transactions.

What's new is Generative AI — AI that creates things instead of just classifying or predicting. ChatGPT, Google Gemini, Claude, and Microsoft Copilot all belong here. So do image generators like DALL·E and Canva's Magic Studio.

The teacher's new toolkit

You don't need to learn every tool. You need to know which 5–6 do 95% of the work.

  • ChatGPT — text generation, lesson planning, explanations, Q&A.
  • Google Gemini — similar to ChatGPT, integrated with Google Workspace.
  • Claude — strong at long, nuanced writing and analysis.
  • Microsoft Copilot — built into Word, PowerPoint, and Teams.
  • Canva Magic Studio — posters, worksheets, slides, classroom visuals.
  • MagicSchool AI — teacher-specific templates (rubrics, IEPs, parent emails).
  • Gamma — turns text into polished slide decks in seconds.
  • Diffit — reading passages at different difficulty levels.

Myths vs realities

⚠ The myth

"AI will replace teachers."

✓ The reality

"AI will replace teachers who refuse to use AI." The skill being tested in the next 5 years isn't whether you know AI exists — it's whether you can use it ethically, efficiently, and in a way that genuinely benefits your students.

Teaching is still — and will remain — a deeply human craft. Pastoral care, classroom culture, mentorship, knowing which child is having a hard week at home — none of that is going anywhere. What's changing is the busywork around teaching: lesson notes, worksheets, parent emails, quiz creation, scheme planning. That's where AI saves you 5–10 hours every week.

Data privacy basics

This is the single most important habit to build from Day 1. Never paste any of the following into a public AI tool:

  • Student full names with grades or behaviour notes attached
  • Confidential health information
  • Parents' phone numbers, addresses, or any personal identifiers
  • Exam questions before the exam has been written
  • Confidential school documents (budgets, staff disputes, board minutes)

If you need help with anything in those categories, use anonymised versions ("Student A scored 35%…") or stick to pen-and-paper.

🎬 Demo — Try this yourself

Open chat.openai.com (free account) and paste this prompt:

"Explain photosynthesis to a Primary 4 pupil in Nigeria using a local example like cassava or mango. Use simple English and a friendly tone."

Notice three things: (1) it responds in seconds, (2) it uses a local example because you asked, (3) it can still be wrong. Always cross-check facts.

Now try the exact same prompt in Gemini and compare the two answers side by side. Which is clearer? Which is more accurate? Which has better local context?

✍ Exercise 1.1 — AI vs You

Pick one topic you taught last week. Ask ChatGPT to explain it to your class level. Compare its explanation to yours. In your workbook, write down:

  • 2 things the AI did better
  • 2 things you did better
  • 1 thing you'd combine

Deliverable: A half-page reflection in your workbook.

Unit summary

  • AI is pattern-recognition software you already use every day.
  • Generative AI creates new text, images, and audio — that's the new shift.
  • Five to six tools (ChatGPT, Gemini, Canva, MagicSchool, Gamma) cover 95% of teacher use cases.
  • Never paste private student data into public AI tools.
  • AI augments, not replaces, your professional judgement.
