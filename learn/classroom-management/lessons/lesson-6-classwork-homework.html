Classwork & Homework Flow

Google Classroom turns the messy cycle of "set work → collect work → mark work → return work" into four clicks. This unit shows you how to post assignments, collect submissions, mark with a rubric, and return feedback in under 30 minutes a week — even with 40 pupils.

⏱ 55 minutes 📊 Beginner 🎯 4 learning objectives

Learning objectives

  1. Create a Google Classroom and invite pupils with a class code.
  2. Post an assignment with instructions, attachments, and a due date.
  3. Collect, mark, and return work using a simple rubric.
  4. Track who has submitted, who is late, and who needs a nudge — at a glance.

Why Google Classroom (and not WhatsApp)

WhatsApp is for parents. Google Classroom is for work. Mixing the two creates chaos: assignments scroll past, photos pile up in your gallery, and you spend Saturday morning matching submissions to names.

Google Classroom solves four problems WhatsApp can't:

  • Organised by pupil. Every submission lands under the pupil's name automatically.
  • Time-stamped. You know exactly who submitted on time, who was late, and who never submitted.
  • One copy per pupil. When you attach a worksheet, each pupil gets their own editable copy. No "Sir, I can't open it" messages at 10 pm.
  • Marks return privately. Pupils see their own grade and feedback — not their classmates'.

15-minute setup

  1. Go to classroom.google.com and sign in with your school Google account (or a personal Gmail if your school has none).
  2. Click the + top-right → Create class. Name it JSS1A — 2026 Session. Add section, subject, and room.
  3. Copy the class code (top of the page). It looks like abc123.
  4. Share the class code with pupils — they go to classroom.google.com, click + → Join class, paste the code.
  5. For pupils without devices: print the code on a slip, ask them to join from a sibling's or parent's phone over the weekend.

Posting an assignment — the 4-step pattern

  1. Classwork tab → Create → Assignment.
  2. Title + instructions. Keep instructions to 3 lines. Example: "Read pages 24–26. Answer questions 1–5 in your own words. Submit by Friday 4 pm."
  3. Attach the worksheet (Drive, PDF, photo of textbook page, or YouTube link). Set "Make a copy for each student" for editable docs.
  4. Due date + points. Set the due date. Set points (usually 10 or 20). Click Assign.

That's it. Every pupil now has the work in their To-do list, with a reminder 24 hours before the due date.

Marking with a simple rubric

A rubric is just a marking scheme written down once and reused forever. Google Classroom lets you attach one to every assignment.

The 3-criterion rubric that works for 80% of subjects:

  • Content (5 pts) — Did they answer the question correctly?
  • Effort (3 pts) — Is the work complete and shows thinking?
  • Presentation (2 pts) — Is it neat, dated, and titled?

To mark: open the assignment → click a pupil's submission → tick rubric levels → type a one-line comment (e.g. "Strong answer on Q3. Reread Q5 — you missed a step.") → click Return. The pupil and parent see the grade instantly.

Tracking submissions at a glance

The assignment page shows three columns:

  • Turned in — submitted on time.
  • Assigned — not yet submitted (red after due date = late).
  • Graded — marked and returned.

Every Friday, open each active assignment and check the Assigned column. Any pupil still in there after due date gets a one-line WhatsApp nudge to the parent: "Bashir's Maths homework was due Wednesday. Please could he submit by Monday?" (See Unit 5 — Template 2.)

👀 Watch it work — Mrs Adeyemi's Friday

Mrs Adeyemi teaches JSS2 English. On Monday morning she posted: "Write a 150-word diary entry as a character from Chapter 4. Due Friday 4 pm."

By Friday 5 pm she opens Google Classroom on her phone:

  • 32 of 38 pupils have submitted (84% — green).
  • 6 pupils still in Assigned: Tunde, Ada, Chinedu, Fatima, Bashir, Ngozi.
  • She opens each submission, ticks the rubric (avg 7/10), types a one-line comment, hits Return. Whole class marked in 28 minutes.
  • Six WhatsApp nudges to parents of non-submitters. Five submit by Monday morning.

Total time: 35 minutes. Last term, without Classroom, this took her three hours and a stack of exercise books to lug home.

✍ Exercise 6.1 — Post your first assignment

Either in your real class or as practice:

  1. Create a Google Classroom for one of your classes.
  2. Post one assignment with: title, 3-line instructions, one attachment, due date, points.
  3. Attach the 3-criterion rubric (Content / Effort / Presentation).
  4. Share the class code with at least 5 pupils (or test colleagues) and ask them to submit.
  5. Mark and return all submissions using the rubric.

Time yourself. Record how long marking took. Most teachers cut their marking time by 60% within two weeks.
